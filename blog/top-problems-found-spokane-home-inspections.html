Top 5 Problems Found in Spokane Home Inspections

Published by Dimitriy · Elevation Inspections · Updated 2025

After hundreds of home inspections across Spokane, Spokane Valley, Liberty Lake, and the surrounding region, certain defects come up again and again. Some are expected in older homes — others surprise buyers who assumed a well-maintained-looking house was problem-free.

Here are the five most common significant findings from Spokane home inspections — and what they mean for you as a buyer.

1

Sewer Line Defects

This is the single most expensive surprise in Spokane home purchases — and the one most easily missed because it's not included in a standard home inspection. Spokane's older neighborhoods are full of clay tile and cast iron sewer lines that are 50–80+ years old.

The most common findings from a camera scope:

  • Root intrusion — tree roots find micro-cracks in old clay pipe and grow inside, eventually blocking flow
  • Bellied pipe — sections that have settled and sag, causing waste to pool
  • Offset joints — pipe sections that have shifted, creating gaps and potential collapse points
  • Cracked or deteriorated pipe — end-of-life clay pipe that crumbles when disturbed

Sewer line replacement in Spokane runs $4,000–$15,000 depending on length, depth, and access. A $175 sewer scope adds this critical check to your inspection. Learn more about sewer scope inspections →

2

Electrical Panel Issues

Spokane has a large stock of homes built in the 1950s–1970s with electrical systems that were never updated. Common findings include:

  • Federal Pacific and Zinsco panels — known defective brands that fail to trip under overload, creating fire risk
  • Double-tapped breakers — two wires on a single breaker not rated for it
  • Undersized service — 60-amp or 100-amp service in homes that now demand 200 amps
  • Aluminum branch circuit wiring — common in 1960s–70s homes, requires special connections and periodic inspection
  • Missing GFCI protection — required in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, and exterior outlets

A panel replacement in Spokane typically runs $2,500–$4,500. If your inspector finds a Federal Pacific "Stab-Lok" panel, get it quoted — many insurance companies won't cover a home with one.

3

Roof Defects and Water Intrusion

Spokane's freeze-thaw cycles, heavy snow loads, and summer heat take a real toll on roofs. A roof that "looks fine" from the curb can have years of deferred damage. Common findings:

  • Worn, curling, or missing shingles — particularly on south- and west-facing slopes
  • Failed flashing at chimneys, skylights, and valleys
  • Deteriorated or improperly installed ice and water shield
  • Evidence of previous leaks in the attic (staining, mold, damaged insulation)
  • Inadequate attic ventilation causing moisture buildup and premature shingle failure

Roof replacement in Spokane runs $8,000–$20,000+ for a typical home. Thermal imaging can reveal active moisture intrusion and staining that's not yet visible from inside.

4

Furnace and HVAC Problems

Eastern Washington winters are cold — and the furnace is critical. Common findings include:

  • Cracked heat exchanger — allows combustion gases including carbon monoxide to enter the living space. A safety concern that warrants immediate attention.
  • End-of-life equipment — furnaces over 20 years old operating on borrowed time
  • Disconnected or leaking ductwork — often found in crawl spaces, dumping conditioned air into unconditioned space
  • Dirty or improperly installed filters — reducing efficiency and equipment life
  • A/C refrigerant issues — common finding on systems that haven't been serviced

A new furnace in Spokane runs $3,000–$6,000 installed. If the inspector finds a cracked heat exchanger, it's a safety issue and a strong negotiating point.

5

Foundation and Water Management Issues

Spokane's hillside topography and clay soils create drainage challenges for many homes. Foundation findings range from cosmetic to structural:

  • Efflorescence — white mineral deposits on foundation walls from water migration (common, watch it)
  • Active moisture intrusion — water entering the basement or crawl space during rain events
  • Grading issues — ground that slopes toward the foundation instead of away from it
  • Horizontal cracks in block foundations — indicates soil pressure and potential structural movement
  • Settlement cracks — diagonal cracks at corners of windows and doors indicating movement

Thermal imaging is especially useful here — moisture inside foundation walls often shows up as cool patches on thermal before visible staining appears.

How to Use This Information

None of these findings are automatically deal-breakers — but each one has a cost. A good inspection report gives you the information to negotiate, budget, or walk away with confidence. The goal isn't to scare you out of buying — it's to make sure you know exactly what you're buying.
