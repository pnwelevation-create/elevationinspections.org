Spokane Radon Levels: What Every Homebuyer Needs to Know

Published by Dimitriy · Elevation Inspections · Updated 2025

If you're buying a home in Spokane, radon testing isn't optional — it's essential. Spokane County sits in EPA Radon Zone 1, which means predicted average indoor radon levels exceed 4 pCi/L, the EPA's recommended action level. You can't see, smell, or taste radon — but it's the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, responsible for about 21,000 deaths per year.

Here's what Spokane homebuyers need to understand before they close.

Why Spokane Has High Radon Levels

Radon comes from the natural decay of uranium in soil and rock. Eastern Washington's geology — including granite-rich soils found in many Spokane neighborhoods — produces elevated radon gas as a byproduct. The gas seeps upward through the soil and enters homes through:

  • Cracks in foundation walls and slabs
  • Utility penetrations (pipes, wires through the foundation)
  • Sump pump pits
  • Crawl space soil
  • Construction joints

Once inside, radon accumulates — especially in lower levels of the home. Basements and ground-floor bedrooms tend to have the highest concentrations.

What Radon Levels Are Typical in Spokane?

Washington State Department of Health data shows Spokane County has significantly elevated radon compared to the national average of 1.3 pCi/L. Testing results in Spokane commonly range from 2–8 pCi/L, with some homes showing levels well above 10 pCi/L in basement spaces.

Rough guideline: About 1 in 6 Spokane homes tested will exceed the EPA's 4 pCi/L action level. In areas with older construction or specific soil types (South Hill, North Side, Spokane Valley), that rate can be higher.

What Happens If Your Radon Test Comes Back High?

A result above 4 pCi/L doesn't mean you can't buy the home — it means you have options:

  • Negotiate a mitigation system: Ask the seller to install a radon mitigation system before closing. This is a reasonable ask and costs $800–$1,500 in Spokane.
  • Request a price reduction: Use the cost of mitigation as a negotiating point — $1,000–$1,500 off the price is fair.
  • Install it yourself post-closing: If you're getting a deal on the home, you may choose to budget for mitigation as part of your move-in costs.

Radon mitigation in Spokane typically involves a sub-slab depressurization system — a certified contractor installs a pipe through the slab and a fan that vents radon gas outside. Most systems reduce levels by 80–99%.

How Radon Testing Works

Short-term radon tests use a small device placed in the lowest livable area of the home for 48–96 hours under closed-house conditions. The device is sent to a certified lab and results come back within a few days. Dimitriy can place a radon test at the same time as the full home inspection — one appointment, all results together.

Does a New Home Need Radon Testing?

Yes. Radon doesn't discriminate by construction date. Many newer Spokane homes are built with passive radon systems (a PVC pipe stubbed through the foundation), but passive systems aren't always effective — they require a fan to actively depressurize. Dimitriy checks whether passive systems are properly installed and whether they need to be activated.
