Sewer Line Problems in Spokane: What Camera Inspections Find

Published by Dimitriy · Elevation Inspections · Updated 2025

Spokane's most expensive home purchase surprise isn't the roof. It isn't the electrical panel. It's the sewer line — the pipe running underground from the house to the city main — which most standard home inspections don't even look at.

Here's what a camera scope actually finds in Spokane's older neighborhoods, and why it matters before you close.

Why Spokane Sewer Lines Are High Risk

Much of Spokane's housing stock — especially in South Hill, Browne's Addition, the North Side, and Spokane Valley — was built between the 1940s and 1980s. Sewer lines installed during that period were typically clay tile or cast iron pipe. Both materials have a lifespan of roughly 50–70 years under normal conditions. That means many Spokane homes have sewer lines that are at or past end of life right now.

Add to that the tree-heavy neighborhoods Spokane is known for — mature elms, pines, and poplars with root systems that actively seek moisture — and you have a recipe for underground problems that nobody sees coming until the toilet stops flushing.

What the Camera Finds

Root Intrusion

The most common finding in Spokane sewer scopes. Tree roots find micro-cracks in clay pipe joints — even hairline cracks — and grow inside the pipe over years. Left unchecked, roots fill the pipe entirely. Minor root intrusion can be hydro-jetted; severe intrusion means replacement.

Bellied Pipe

Sections of pipe that have settled and sag downward. Waste pools in the low point rather than flowing to the main. Over time, this creates chronic blockages. Belly repairs typically require excavation and resetting the affected section.

Offset Joints

Pipe sections that have shifted apart at the joint — either laterally or vertically. Creates a ledge where waste catches, allows root entry, and can collapse entirely under load. Common in homes with significant soil movement or tree root pressure.

Cracked and Deteriorated Pipe

Old clay pipe doesn't crack cleanly — it fractures, spalls, and crumbles. At some point, a deteriorated section becomes a collapse risk. The camera shows exactly where the pipe wall is compromised.

Grease and Scale Buildup

Common in homes with cast iron pipe. Decades of grease accumulation coats the inside of the pipe, narrowing the channel. Less urgent than structural failures, but a sign of a pipe that needs professional cleaning.

Real cost context: Sewer line replacement in Spokane typically costs $4,000–$8,000 for a standard run. Longer runs, deep lines, or lines under concrete can push $12,000–$15,000+. A $175 scope inspection before closing is one of the best dollars you'll spend.

What Happens When Problems Are Found

A sewer scope finding gives you real negotiating leverage:

  • Ask the seller to repair or replace — major findings (root intrusion, offset joints, collapsed sections) are legitimate repair requests
  • Negotiate a price reduction — use a contractor quote for the repair cost as the basis
  • Walk away — a sewer line in poor condition on an already-stressed budget is a reasonable deal-breaker

Minor findings (light root tendrils, minimal grease buildup) often don't require negotiation — they're maintenance items you can handle post-closing for a few hundred dollars.
